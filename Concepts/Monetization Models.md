# Monetization Models

How launchers earn shapes what they build (axis 6 in [[Launcher Mental Models]]). See concrete numbers in [[Pricing Comparison]], and the acquisition side in [[Growth and Marketing Strategies]].

## 1. One-time unlock ("Prime / Pro")
Pay once, own the premium feature set. Classic indie model.
- **Pros**: aligned incentives (build a good product, sell it); no recurring friction; users feel ownership.
- **Cons**: revenue is front-loaded; hard to fund years of ongoing development from a single sale; pressure to ship paid "2.0" versions.
- **Examples**: [[Nova Launcher]] (Prime), [[Action Launcher]] (Plus), historically most indie launchers.

## 2. Subscription
Recurring fee, often with a lifetime option.
- **Pros**: funds continuous development, sync, cloud features; predictable revenue.
- **Cons**: subscription fatigue; users resist paying monthly for a home screen; needs to keep justifying value.
- **Examples**: [[Niagara Launcher]] (Pro, yearly or lifetime), [[Smart Launcher]] (offers both).

## 3. Free / open source (FOSS)
No price; funded by donations, volunteers, or nothing.
- **Pros**: trust, no upsell, auditable (matters for [[Privacy and Launchers]]); community contributions.
- **Cons**: slower or inconsistent development; bus-factor risk; no support guarantee.
- **Examples**: [[Lawnchair]], [[KISS Launcher]], [[Olauncher]].

## 4. Vendor-funded / strategic
Free because it serves a larger business — drives ecosystem engagement or sells devices.
- **Microsoft**: [[Microsoft Launcher]] pulls Android users into Microsoft 365 / Windows continuity.
- **Google**: [[Pixel Launcher]] sells Pixel hardware and surfaces Google services / Search.
- **OEMs**: [[OEM Launchers]] are part of the device's value prop (and sometimes ad-monetised, e.g. MIUI historically).
- Incentive risk: feeds and ads can conflict with a clean experience.

## 5. Ads / recommendation feeds
Less common in premium launchers, common in OEM defaults. Monetises attention, which directly conflicts with the [[Digital Wellbeing and Minimalism]] philosophy.

## The tension
A launcher is the **most-used, least-reconfigured** surface on the phone. That makes it sticky (high [[Launcher Mental Models|switching cost]]) but hard to monetise repeatedly — users set it up once and never want to think about it again. Subscriptions fight that instinct; one-time unlocks fit it but starve long-term funding. The Nova/Branch saga (acquisition, layoffs, uncertainty, later spun back out) is the cautionary tale of this fragility.
