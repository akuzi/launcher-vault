# Digital Wellbeing and Minimalism

The thesis behind a whole class of launchers: **the home screen is a behaviour-shaping surface**, and redesigning it can reduce compulsive phone use.

## The mechanism (the bet these launchers make)
Compulsive opens are largely *cue-driven* and *unconscious*. The home screen supplies the cues:
- **Colorful icons** act as visual triggers (the Instagram gradient, the red YouTube play button).
- **Spatial muscle memory** lets you open a time-sink app without conscious intent — thumb goes there automatically.
- **Badges / notification dots** manufacture urgency.

Minimalist launchers attack each cue:
- **Remove color** → text-only or monochrome lists ([[Olauncher]], [[Before Launcher]]).
- **Remove fixed positions** → search or alphabetical lists break muscle memory ([[Niagara Launcher]], [[KISS Launcher]]).
- **Add intentional friction** → an extra gesture/search step forces a beat of conscious choice (axis 2 in [[Launcher Mental Models]]).
- **Hide most apps** → only a handful of "essentials" visible.

## Spectrum of strictness
1. **Soft minimal** — clean grid, fewer icons, muted icon pack. Still spatial.
2. **List-based** — [[Niagara Launcher]]: short list, one-handed, pop-out menu.
3. **Text-only** — [[Olauncher]]: a few words on a blank screen, no drawer, no icons.
4. **Hardcore** — paired with grayscale mode, app timers, locked folders.

## Complementary OS features
- **Android Digital Wellbeing** — screen-time dashboard, app timers, Focus mode, grayscale "Bedtime" mode.
- **Grayscale** — desaturating the whole display removes color cues system-wide; pairs powerfully with a minimalist launcher.

## Critiques / open questions
- Friction can be *routed around* — determined users just adapt; the muscle memory reforms.
- Some research suggests reducing *cues* helps more than reducing *access*.
- A minimalist launcher does nothing about in-app design (infinite scroll, autoplay).
- Novelty effect: benefits may fade after the setup honeymoon.
